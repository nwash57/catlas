TNR basics

How to start a TNR program

You don't need an organization behind you. Most TNR programs start with one person who noticed the same cats every day and decided to do something about it. Here's how to go from noticing to managing.

Step 1: Count and map the colony

Before you trap anything, spend a week observing. Visit the feeding area at the same time each day — ideally morning and dusk when cats are most active. Note:

  • How many cats, and rough descriptions (color, markings, size)
  • Which already have an ear tip — they're done, don't re-trap them
  • Any kittens, and approximately how old (under 8 weeks, 8–16 weeks, older)
  • Where the cats shelter, sleep, and enter/exit the area

A rough headcount and map is your baseline. You'll use it to know when you've caught everyone and to track progress over time.

Step 2: Find a clinic before you trap anything

Never trap a cat without a confirmed surgery appointment. Cats held in traps for more than 24 hours become dangerously stressed. Most low-cost clinics book out days or weeks in advance, so this step comes before you own a single trap.

Look for clinics that explicitly offer TNR services — they're familiar with feral temperament, move efficiently on surgery day, and typically charge a flat TNR rate rather than a full exam fee. Many have holding protocols for overnight recovery so you don't need to manage a post-surgery cat at home.

Step 3: Get traps

Standard drop-trap or box trap, 30–36 inches long. Tomahawk and Tru-Catch are reliable brands. You don't need to buy them — many humane societies, TNR organizations, and animal control agencies lend traps for free. Call around before spending money.

Plan for one trap per cat you intend to catch, plus a spare. Catching three cats at once with one trap means two cats are waiting in the open while you make trips, which is inefficient and stressful.

Step 4: Withhold food the night before

Cats enter traps for food. A well-fed cat has no reason to go in. Stop feeding the colony 12–24 hours before your trapping session. This is uncomfortable to do but it's the single biggest factor in trap success rate.

Step 5: Set traps and stay close

Place traps near the feeding area, ideally against a wall or fence where cats feel sheltered. Bait with a small amount of strong-smelling food — canned tuna, sardines, or the cat's regular wet food — at the back of the trap. Cover the trap with a towel or sheet, leaving the entrance open; a covered trap feels like shelter and reduces stress once a cat is inside.

Do not leave set traps unattended. A trapped cat can injure itself trying to escape, overheat in direct sun, or attract the attention of people who might interfere. Check traps every 15–30 minutes. Once a cat is caught, cover the trap completely, move it somewhere quiet and shaded, and reset for the next cat.

Step 6: Transport and surgery day

Transport cats in the trap — don't transfer them to a carrier. Keep the trap covered, avoid loud music, and drive smoothly. At the clinic, they'll handle the rest: surgery, ear tip, rabies vaccine, and a basic health check.

Ask the clinic about their post-surgery protocol. Most will keep cats overnight and ask you to pick up the next morning. The cat should be alert and standing before return.

Step 7: Recovery and return

After pickup, keep the cat in the closed trap in a quiet, temperature-controlled space for 24–48 hours. Offer water and a small amount of food through the trap door. Don't open the trap — a cat that escapes indoors post-surgery is extremely difficult to recapture and may injure itself or you trying.

Return the cat to the exact spot where it was trapped. This is their territory; they know it. Open the trap door and step back. Most cats will bolt immediately — that's normal and fine.

Step 8: Document and keep going

After each trapping session, update your colony record: which cats were done, date, clinic used, any health notes. Photos help — a cat you don't recognize may already be ear-tipped and just hard to see clearly at distance.

Aim to reach 70–80% sterilization of the colony as quickly as you can. The sooner you cross that threshold, the sooner the population stops growing. Front-loading the effort pays off faster than a slow drip of one cat per month.

Common first-timer mistakes

  • Trapping without a clinic appointment. The cat is in the trap. Now what? Book first, trap second.
  • Releasing too early. A groggy cat released immediately after surgery can't defend itself. The 24–48 hour recovery hold matters.
  • Re-trapping ear-tipped cats. Check for ear tips before every session. A trap with an already-sterilized cat is a wasted clinic slot and an unnecessary stress on the cat.
  • Giving up after one hard session. Trapping is inconsistent. Some nights you catch six cats in two hours; some nights you catch none. Persistence over weeks matters more than any single session.
  • Working alone forever. Find one other person — a neighbor, a friend — who can cover a session when you can't. Burnout is the most common reason TNR programs stall.
