TNR basics

Why TNR works

TNR's effectiveness comes down to two mechanisms: it breaks the reproductive cycle faster than cat immigration can replace it, and it removes the incentive for new cats to move in. Neither trap-and-remove nor doing nothing achieves both.

The vacuum effect — why removal backfires

Outdoor cats are territorial. When a group of cats occupies a feeding area, they actively discourage newcomers. Remove those cats — trap them, relocate them, or euthanize them — and the territory opens up. Intact cats from surrounding areas detect the vacancy through the absence of scent marking and fighting, and move in within weeks to months.

The new arrivals are unsterilized. Breeding resumes. Because feral cats can have two to three litters per year, a cleared colony can repopulate to its original size in a single breeding season.

TNR sidesteps the vacuum effect because the sterilized cats stay. They hold the territory, eat the available food, and repel newcomers — but produce no kittens. The colony shrinks through natural attrition rather than through replacement.

The 80% threshold

Population models consistently find that a colony stops growing — and begins declining — once roughly 70–80% of the females are sterilized.1 Below that threshold, the remaining intact females produce enough surviving kittens to offset attrition. Above it, even accounting for some immigration, the population trends downward.

This is why studies showing TNR "doesn't work" typically involve low sterilization coverage, open settings with heavy immigration pressure, or programs that ran for only a few years.2 The biology isn't the variable — implementation is.

What the long-term data shows

The clearest evidence comes from closed or semi-closed populations where caretakers maintained consistent effort over many years:

  • A 28-year program at the University of Central Florida enrolled 204 cats across 16 colonies. By the end, the population had declined 85% and 11 of 16 colonies were entirely eliminated.3
  • A 16-year targeted program in the San Francisco Bay Area enrolled 258 cats and achieved a 99.4% population decline. Zero kittens were born after the first six years of the program.4
  • A Florida Keys study tracked colonies over 11 years; those receiving sustained TNR declined significantly while unmanaged colonies grew.5

The common thread: high sterilization rates, consistent colony monitoring, and duration long enough for attrition to compound. A two-year sprint followed by abandonment does not produce the same result as a decade of steady caretaking.

Why cats return to the same territory

Returning cats after surgery isn't sentiment — it's strategy. Cats form strong attachments to familiar territories and become stressed or disoriented when relocated. More importantly, an established cat knows the local food sources, shelter spots, and social hierarchy. That knowledge makes it an effective occupant who holds the territory against new arrivals.

Relocated cats often disappear within days of release in unfamiliar areas, creating another vacancy. Some shelters have moved to managed TNR specifically because relocation had a worse long-term outcome than return.

The behavioral dividend

Sterilization changes behavior in ways that reduce nuisance complaints — often the political barrier to keeping programs funded. Intact males roam widely to find mates, fight for territory, and yowl. After neutering, roaming distance drops, fights decrease, and mating vocalizations stop. Spraying frequency also declines. Neighbors who were vocal opponents of a colony sometimes become neutral or supportive once the behavioral complaints disappear.6

The honest limits

TNR is not effective in every situation. Very open environments — large rural areas without defined feeding stations, or parks with a constant influx of abandoned cats — can have immigration rates that outpace even high sterilization coverage. In those settings, complementary strategies (municipal adoption pipelines, targeted kitten socialization, community education about abandonment) are necessary alongside TNR. Conservation biologists have raised legitimate concerns about TNR's pace relative to wildlife protection goals — a real tension, not a dismissed one.7

The proposed alternatives, however, don't hold up either. Lethal trap-and-remove produces the vacuum effect described above and has never demonstrated a sustained population reduction at scale. Mass impoundment of feral cats strains shelter resources without improving outcomes: unsocialized adults are not adoptable, and holding them diverts space and staff from cats that can be rehomed. Doing nothing is not a neutral choice — it leaves colonies to grow unchecked and caretakers without any framework.

Catlas is built on the conviction that the humane path is also the practical one. We don't believe in killing cats as a management strategy, and we don't think shelters should absorb the cost of a problem that TNR and community caretaking can address in the field. Our focus is on giving caretakers better tools so that more colonies are managed well — because a well-managed colony, stable and sterilized, is better for the cats, the community, and the ecosystem than the alternative of nothing.
